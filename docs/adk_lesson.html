01 · The Question Why not just one big agent? —
The premise

One brain can think.
But can it argue
with itself?

A single LLM call gives you one perspective, one chain of reasoning. The moment your problem needs debate, negotiation, specialization, or collaboration — you need more than one mind.

Today we'll feel why this matters. Not by hearing about it — by watching agents lie, accuse, and unmask each other in real time.

02 · Definition What is a Multi-Agent System? —
Concept

A team of autonomous minds
solving a problem together.

Imagine you walked into a hospital with a strange illness. You don't just see one doctor. You see a receptionist, who routes you. A general physician, who diagnoses broadly. A specialist, who goes deep. A pharmacist, who acts. A nurse, who coordinates.

No one person has the full picture. But they pass messages — your chart, your symptoms, prescriptions — and together they solve a problem none of them could solve alone.

That's a multi-agent system. The hospital, not the doctor.

Three things make it real:

  1. Autonomy — each agent decides what to do next, on its own.
  2. Specialization — each agent is good at one slice of the problem.
  3. Communication — they pass information to each other to make progress.
03 · Core Principle Problem Solving by Message Passing —
The heart of everything

Intelligence is what happens
between the agents.

When you build a multi-agent system, you don't build "smart agents." You build a conversation. The cleverness lives in the messages — what gets said, who hears it, what gets remembered, what stays private.

Change the messages and the system becomes a different thing entirely. Same agents. Same prompts. Different outcomes.

04 · Warm-up Emergence — the magic of many simple minds —
Before we get to language

Watch fifty ants find a path
that no single ant knows.

Each ant is dumb. It can only do three things: walk, smell pheromones, drop pheromones. It has no map. It has never seen the food. It cannot remember more than its last few steps.

And yet — a colony of these ants finds the shortest path between nest and food, every single time. Not because any ant is clever. Because of messages they leave for each other on the ground.

  • The medium is the message. Pheromone strength = information.
  • No central controller. The path emerges from local interactions.
  • Stigmergy — the technical name. Agents communicating through the environment.
  • This is multi-agent thinking at its purest.

Now imagine if each "ant" could also talk, reason, and plan. That's where we're going.

04.1 · Emergence When the whole exceeds the sum —
The principle behind every multi-agent system

More is different.

Hydrogen burns. Oxygen burns. Combine them — and you get a liquid that puts out fire. Nothing in either atom contains "wetness." It appears only when they interact.

On the right, four rules govern each cell: stay alive with 2 or 3 neighbors, die of loneliness, die of overcrowding, be born from exactly three. That's it. No rule mentions movement. No rule mentions shape. And yet — gliders are born. Patterns hunt. Computation emerges.

  • No agent knows the pattern. The cells, the termites, the neurons — none of them can see the structure they create.
  • It lives at a level no agent inhabits. You can only see it from outside the system.
  • Cannot be reduced. You can travel from local rules up to global pattern. You can never go back down.

Termites build cathedrals. Neurons think thoughts. Agents — when we get the local rules right — solve problems none of them could solve alone.

04.2 · Sensitivity The butterfly effect —
Why your debugger won't save you

A whisper of difference,
a wildly different world.

Two pendulums. Indistinguishable start positions — separated by one part in a thousand of a radian. For a few seconds they trace the same arc. Then they don't. Then they look like different systems entirely.

This is the signature of a complex system. Tiny causes have outsized effects. Same agents, same rules — but a microscopic difference in starting state leads to a wildly different outcome.

Complicated

A jet engine. 25,000 parts. Same inputs → same outputs. Decomposable. Your debugger works.

Complex

A flock. A market. A village of agents. Sensitive to starting conditions. Your debugger won't save you.

Run our mafia village twice with the same agents — and you will get two different stories. That is not a bug. That is the system being alive.

Δθ ≈ 0.001 rad · two universes

06 · The Framework Google Agent Development Kit — Mental Map —
The whole framework, in one analogy

ADK is a call center for agents.

ADK is to AI agents what a web framework is to HTTP — you don't reinvent routing, middleware, or sessions, you assemble them. Picture a call center. Every piece below is something ADK already names.

07 · LlmAgent The worker — primitive 1 of 7 —
Primitive #1

LlmAgent. The single thinking unit.

An LlmAgent (alias Agent) is an LLM-powered worker. You give it four things and it knows what to do.

  • name — the identifier other agents call it by.
  • model — the brain. gemini-2.5-flash, claude-sonnet, anything you've configured.
  • instruction — the personality. Its system prompt. The whole reason you hired this worker.
  • description — its job posting. Other agents read this to decide whether to hand off work.

Gotcha: description matters more than you think. In multi-agent setups, vague descriptions cause wrong handoffs. Write it like a job listing, not a code comment.

# The 95% case. This is what you'll write first. from google.adk.agents import Agent from google.adk.tools import google_search root_agent = Agent( name="weather_assistant", model="gemini-2.5-flash", description="Answers weather questions.", instruction="You help users check the weather.", tools=[google_search], )

Use cases: chatbots, research assistants, support agents, code reviewers, anything that needs to think. 95% of agents you build start here.

08 · Tools Hands — primitive 2 of 7 —
Primitive #2

An agent can think. Tools let it act.

A Tool is anything the LLM can call when reasoning isn't enough — an API, a database query, sending an email, casting a vote.

# Any function with type hints + docstring = a tool. def cast_vote(player: str) -> dict: """Record this agent's vote against `player`. Args: player: name of the suspected wolf. Returns: dict with status and current tally.""" tally[player] += 1 return {"status": "ok", "tally": tally} agent = Agent(name="aruna", model="gemini-2.5-flash", instruction="Cast your vote each day.", tools=[cast_vote])

Gotcha: the docstring IS the prompt. The LLM reads it to decide when to call the tool. Vague docstring = wrong calls.

Four flavors of tools

FunctionTool

Your own Python function. Schema auto-generated from type hints + docstring. Anything the LLM can't do natively.

Built-in tools

google_search, built_in_code_execution, load_memory, load_artifacts — import and use.

AgentTool

Call another agent as if it were a function. The other agent answers; control returns to you.

Adapters · MCP & OpenAPI

Point at any MCP server (Notion, GitHub, Slack...) or Swagger spec — ADK auto-generates the toolset.

09 · Workflow Agents Choreographers — primitive 3 of 7 —
Primitive #3

When the flow is known, don't ask the LLM.

Workflow agents run sub-agents in a fixed pattern. The content at each step is still LLM-driven; the control flow is hardcoded. Cheaper, faster, more predictable than letting the LLM orchestrate.

SequentialAgent

The Assembly Line

A → B → C. Output of step N feeds step N+1. ETL, draft → review → polish flows.

ParallelAgent

The Fan-Out

A, B, C all run at once. Independent sub-tasks. Saves wall-clock time.

LoopAgent

The Refinement Cycle

Run, critique, run again. Until quality passes or max iterations hit.

10 · State & Memory Sticky note vs filing cabinet — primitive 4 of 7 —
Primitive #4

A sticky note, and a filing cabinet.

State

The sticky note

A dict the agent and tools read and write during one conversation. Slots in a form, intermediate results between sub-agents, choices the user made this turn.

# Inside a tool: tool_context.state["vote"] = "aruna" # Key prefixes pick the scope: state["key"] # this session state["user:k"] # this user, across sessions state["app:k"] # everyone using this app state["temp:k"] # this turn only
Memory

The filing cabinet

A separate service for "what does this agent remember about this user, across sessions?" Backed by InMemoryMemoryService for dev, VertexAiMemoryBankService for prod.

from google.adk.tools import load_memory agent = Agent( name="personal_assistant", model="gemini-2.5-flash", instruction="Use load_memory to recall past info about the user.", tools=[load_memory], )

In our village: each agent's role is private state, the public log is session state, "who won the last 10 games" is memory.

11 · Callbacks Middleware — primitive 5 of 7 —
Primitive #5

The guardrails. The audit log.
The middleware.

Functions that fire at specific lifecycle points: before/after the agent runs, before/after the model is called, before/after a tool fires. Think Express middleware or Django signals.

Use them for anything that needs to run around the agent, not inside it: guardrails, logging, caching, rate limits, PII redaction, observability.

before_agent → before_model → model → after_model → before_tool → tool → after_tool

Mafia use case: a before_model_callback scrubs leaked role names from a wolf's public message. An after_tool_callback logs every vote to the audit trail.

def block_pii(callback_context, llm_request): """Run before each model call — scrub PII.""" text = llm_request.contents[-1].parts[0].text if "ssn" in text.lower(): return LlmResponse( content=Content(parts=[Part(text= "I can't process that.")])) return None # proceed normally agent = Agent( name="safe_agent", model="gemini-2.5-flash", instruction="Help the user.", before_model_callback=block_pii, )

Return None to let the request through; return a response object to short-circuit and override.

12 · Runner & Events The engine and its output — primitives 6 & 7 of 7 —
Primitives #6 & #7

The engine that runs everything,
and the stream it emits.

The Runner is what actually executes your agent. adk web, adk run, and adk api_server all give you Runners for free. For production embedding, you wire it yourself.

It yields a stream of Events — every "thing that happened." A session is just an ordered list of events.

text agent or user speech
function_call agent invokes a tool
function_response tool result returns
state_delta state was mutated

A streaming UI, a custom dashboard, a replayable transcript — it all comes from the event stream.

from google.adk.runners import InMemoryRunner from google.genai import types runner = InMemoryRunner(agent=root_agent, app_name="mafia") session = await runner.session_service.create_session( app_name="mafia", user_id="ashan" ) msg = types.Content(role="user", parts=[types.Part(text="Begin round.")]) async for event in runner.run_async( user_id="ashan", session_id=session.id, new_message=msg, ): if event.is_final_response(): print(event.content.parts[0].text)

For today's build, adk web handles the Runner for us. We just write the agents.

13 · Beyond the 80% What waits past the basics —
Not today — but worth knowing

When the basics stop being enough.

The seven primitives we just covered solve the 80% case. Here's the other 20% — for when you outgrow them.

Artifacts

Files & binary data

Versioned blobs tied to a session — generated images, PDFs, CSV exports. Don't shove binaries into state.

A2A Protocol

Remote agents

One agent calls another over the network — possibly in a different language, by a different team. Enterprise agent meshes.

Plugins

App-wide behaviors

Pre-bundled callbacks + setup applied to every agent in your app. Logging, tracing, BigQuery exporters.

Evaluation

Don't ship vibes

Eval sets (.evalset.json) + adk eval. Regression tests for agents. Compare prompts, models, tool accuracy.

Deployment

Where agents live

adk deploy agent_engine (managed Vertex), cloud_run, or gke. Start with Agent Engine.

Long-Running Tools

Human-in-the-loop

LongRunningFunctionTool — async tools that pause an agent and resume when a human approves.

Today we use the 80%. Tomorrow, when these become useful, you'll know where to look.

14 · Today's Build The Mafia Village Simulation —
What we're building

Eight agents.
A hidden truth.
One survivor.

We're going to build a multi-agent simulation of the social deduction game Mafia (sometimes called Werewolf). Some agents secretly know they're wolves. The rest are villagers trying to figure out who. Every round they talk, accuse, vote — and someone dies.

Why this? Because it requires every single thing we just learned. LlmAgents with secret roles. Tools to cast votes. Sequential phases. Parallel reasoning. A LoopAgent until someone wins. Callbacks to keep wolves honest. And the agents will genuinely surprise you.

14.1 · Setup The Cast —
Eight roles · Eight agents

Who lives in our village?

W

Wolf

×2 · Hidden

Kills one villager each night. Knows the other wolf. Must lie convincingly by day.

S

Seer

×1 · Hidden

Each night, secretly learns whether one chosen player is a wolf. Knows truth but must protect their identity.

D

Doctor

×1 · Hidden

Each night, secretly heals one player. If they pick the wolf's target, that player survives.

V

Villager

×4 · Knows nothing

No special powers. Only weapon is observation, debate, and the vote. The honest majority.

Each agent gets two slices of state

Public log — what was said in the village square. Session-scoped state, everyone reads it.

Private notepad — what only this agent knows. Their role, their observations, their secrets. Stored under user:-prefixed keys. No other agent can read this.

Win conditions

Wolves win when they equal or outnumber villagers.

Villagers win when both wolves are voted out.

Nobody knows the outcome in advance. Not even us.

14.2 · The Loop Day · Night · Repeat —
The orchestration

Every round, the village breathes.

Now the primitives line up. A LoopAgent runs each round until a win condition fires. Each round is a SequentialAgent with two phases — and inside the night phase a ParallelAgent lets the wolves, seer, and doctor act at the same time.

Night phase

Wolves message each other privately to pick a target. Seer checks one player. Doctor heals one player. All in parallel — none of them see the others' moves.

Day phase

A moderator announces the death (if any). Each surviving villager speaks publicly — accusations, defenses, theories. Then everyone votes. The accused dies.

A before_model callback scrubs role names from the wolves' public speech. Tools record every vote. The Runner streams every event — accusation, lie, deduction — to your terminal. This is what we'll watch unfold.
