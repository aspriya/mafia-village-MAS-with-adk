01 · The Question Why not just one big agent? —
The premise

One brain can think.
But can it argue
with itself?

A single LLM call gives you one perspective, one chain of reasoning. The moment your problem needs debate, negotiation, specialization, or collaboration — you need more than one mind.

Today we'll feel why this matters. Not by hearing about it — by watching agents lie, accuse, and unmask each other in real time.

02 · Definition What is a Multi-Agent System? —
Concept

A team of autonomous minds
solving a problem together.

Imagine you walked into a hospital with a strange illness. You don't just see one doctor. You see a receptionist, who routes you. A general physician, who diagnoses broadly. A specialist, who goes deep. A pharmacist, who acts. A nurse, who coordinates.

No one person has the full picture. But they pass messages — your chart, your symptoms, prescriptions — and together they solve a problem none of them could solve alone.

That's a multi-agent system. The hospital, not the doctor.

Three things make it real:

  1. Autonomy — each agent decides what to do next, on its own.
  2. Specialization — each agent is good at one slice of the problem.
  3. Communication — they pass information to each other to make progress.
03 · Core Principle Problem Solving by Message Passing —
The heart of everything

Intelligence is what happens
between the agents.

When you build a multi-agent system, you don't build "smart agents." You build a conversation. The cleverness lives in the messages — what gets said, who hears it, what gets remembered, what stays private.

Change the messages and the system becomes a different thing entirely. Same agents. Same prompts. Different outcomes.

04 · The Tool Google Agent Development Kit —
The Framework

You bring the conversation.

Google's Agent Development Kit gives us three primitives. Master these and you can compose almost any multi-agent system.

01

LlmAgent

A single thinking unit. Has instructions (its personality), a model (its brain), tools (its hands), and a name. This is your specialist.

02

Workflow Agents

Orchestrators that don't think themselves — they choreograph others. Sequential, Parallel, Loop. The conductors.

The shared whiteboard. Every agent reads from and writes to it. This is how messages persist across an agent run.

05 · Orchestration Three ways agents can flow —
Workflow patterns

How do you choreograph a team?

SequentialAgent

The Assembly Line

A → B → C. Each agent finishes, hands off to the next. Best for pipelines.

ParallelAgent

The Fan-Out

A, B, C all run at once. Best when their work is independent. Faster.

LoopAgent

The Refinement Cycle

Run, critique, run again. Until quality passes or max iterations hit.

06 · Warm-up Emergence — the magic of many simple minds —
Before we get to language

Watch fifty ants find a path
that no single ant knows.

Each ant is dumb. It can only do three things: walk, smell pheromones, drop pheromones. It has no map. It has never seen the food. It cannot remember more than its last few steps.

And yet — a colony of these ants finds the shortest path between nest and food, every single time. Not because any ant is clever. Because of messages they leave for each other on the ground.

  • The medium is the message. Pheromone strength = information.
  • No central controller. The path emerges from local interactions.
  • Stigmergy — the technical name. Agents communicating through the environment.
  • This is multi-agent thinking at its purest.

Now imagine if each "ant" could also talk, reason, and plan. That's where we're going.

06.1 · Emergence When the whole exceeds the sum —
The principle behind every multi-agent system

More is different.

Hydrogen burns. Oxygen burns. Combine them — and you get a liquid that puts out fire. Nothing in either atom contains "wetness." It appears only when they interact.

On the right, four rules govern each cell: stay alive with 2 or 3 neighbors, die of loneliness, die of overcrowding, be born from exactly three. That's it. No rule mentions movement. No rule mentions shape. And yet — gliders are born. Patterns hunt. Computation emerges.

  • No agent knows the pattern. The cells, the termites, the neurons — none of them can see the structure they create.
  • It lives at a level no agent inhabits. You can only see it from outside the system.
  • Cannot be reduced. You can travel from local rules up to global pattern. You can never go back down.

Termites build cathedrals. Neurons think thoughts. Agents — when we get the local rules right — solve problems none of them could solve alone.

06.2 · Sensitivity The butterfly effect —
Why your debugger won't save you

A whisper of difference,
a wildly different world.

Two pendulums. Indistinguishable start positions — separated by one part in a thousand of a radian. For a few seconds they trace the same arc. Then they don't. Then they look like different systems entirely.

This is the signature of a complex system. Tiny causes have outsized effects. Same agents, same rules — but a microscopic difference in starting state leads to a wildly different outcome.

Complicated

A jet engine. 25,000 parts. Same inputs → same outputs. Decomposable. Your debugger works.

Complex

A flock. A market. A village of agents. Sensitive to starting conditions. Your debugger won't save you.

Run our mafia village twice with the same agents — and you will get two different stories. That is not a bug. That is the system being alive.

Δθ ≈ 0.001 rad · two universes

08 · Today's Build The Mafia Village Simulation —
What we're building

Eight agents.
A hidden truth.
One survivor.

We're going to build a multi-agent simulation of the social deduction game Mafia (sometimes called Werewolf). Some agents secretly know they're wolves. The rest are villagers trying to figure out who. Every round they talk, accuse, vote — and someone dies.

Why this? Because it requires every single thing we just learned. Private state. Public state. Sequential phases. Parallel reasoning. Loop until win condition. And the agents will genuinely surprise you.

08.1 · Setup The Cast —
Eight roles · Eight agents

Who lives in our village?

W

Wolf

×2 · Hidden

Kills one villager each night. Knows the other wolf. Must lie convincingly by day.

S

Seer

×1 · Hidden

Each night, secretly learns whether one chosen player is a wolf. Knows truth but must protect their identity.

D

Doctor

×1 · Hidden

Each night, secretly heals one player. If they pick the wolf's target, that player survives.

V

Villager

×4 · Knows nothing

No special powers. Only weapon is observation, debate, and the vote. The honest majority.

Each agent gets two memories

Public memory — what was said in the village square. Everyone reads this.

Private memory — what only this agent knows. Their role, their observations, their secrets. No other agent can read this.

Win conditions

Wolves win when they equal or outnumber villagers.

Villagers win when both wolves are voted out.

Nobody knows the outcome in advance. Not even us.

08.2 · The Loop Day · Night · Repeat —
The orchestration

Every round, the village breathes.

The LoopAgent runs each round until a win condition fires. Each round is a SequentialAgent with two phases:

Night phase

Wolves message each other privately to pick a target. Seer checks one player. Doctor heals one player. All in parallel — none of them see the others' moves.

Day phase

A moderator announces the death (if any). Each surviving villager speaks publicly — accusations, defenses, theories. Then everyone votes. The accused dies.

A wolf trying to look innocent. A seer who knows the truth but can't reveal themselves without dying. A doctor guessing who to save. This is what we'll watch unfold.
